Who chose your electricity supplier in Spain?

25 April 2026 · 4 min read · electricity supplier expat guide Spain switching tariff

When you completed on your property, a lot was happening at once. Paperwork, notaries, money moving in every direction. Your lawyer had a dozen things to manage. Somewhere in the middle of all that, the electricity got transferred into your name.

Did anyone ask which supplier you wanted?

No. They didn't.

What your lawyer actually did

There's a standard process for this. It's called a cambio de titularidad — a holder change. Your name goes where the previous owner's was. Same supplier, same tariff, same contracted power. Takes a few minutes. Done.

Nobody compares tariffs. Nobody checks whether the power capacity suits how you'll use the place. Your lawyer's job is to complete the sale, not audit your energy contract. So they do the quickest thing and move on. That's their job. It just means nobody did yours.

Why the default is usually expensive

The big legacy suppliers — Iberdrola, Endesa — are where most properties end up. They're the names developers defaulted to when buildings were first connected to the grid. They get inherited again and again as properties change hands.

They're also considerably more expensive than the smaller providers. The gap has grown over the last few years. If you've never looked at this, you're probably paying more than you need to.

The charge that runs when nobody's home

Most people think of their electricity bill as one thing — you use power, you pay for it. But there are actually two separate charges. One is for consumption. The other is a daily charge based on your contracted capacity, meaning how much power your connection is set up to deliver at any moment. That second charge runs every day whether you're in the property or not.

Developers set this figure when a building is first connected, and they always go high. More capacity means fewer complaints. Then it just sits there, passing from owner to owner.

A two-bedroom apartment typically needs around 3 kilowatts. A lot of them are contracted at 5.75. That gap costs money daily, and most people have no idea it's there. We've written about this in more detail here.

What to actually look at

Pull out your most recent electricity bill. Three things are worth checking.

  • Who your supplier is. If you've never actively chosen them, you inherited them.
  • The potencia figure near the top of the bill — that's your contracted capacity in kilowatts. Your bill also shows the maximum power you actually drew during the period. If those numbers are far apart, you're paying for something you're not using.
  • The tariff type. Either a flat price all day, or three different prices depending on the hour. Neither is universally better. It depends entirely on when you use electricity.

Nothing physical happens when you switch supplier in Spain. Same cables, same meter, same distributor. You're just changing who invoices you.
